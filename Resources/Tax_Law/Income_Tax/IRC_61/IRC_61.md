# IRC § 61 – Gross Income Defined

**Source:** [Legal Information Institute – 26 U.S.C. § 61](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/61)

## Text of the Statute

> **§ 61. Gross income defined**  
> Except as otherwise provided in this subtitle, gross income means all income from whatever source derived, including (but not limited to) the following items:
> 
> (1) Compensation for services, including fees, commissions, fringe benefits, and similar items;  
> (2) Gross income derived from business;  
> (3) Gains derived from dealings in property;  
> (4) Interest;  
> (5) Rents;  
> (6) Royalties;  
> (7) Dividends;  
> (8) Alimony and separate maintenance payments;  
> (9) Annuities;  
> (10) Income from life insurance and endowment contracts;  
> (11) Pensions;  
> (12) Income from discharge of indebtedness;  
> (13) Distributive share of partnership gross income;  
> (14) Income in respect of a decedent; and  
> (15) Income from an interest in an estate or trust.

## Summary

- **Broad definition:** “All income from whatever source derived” establishes a sweeping baseline for federal income tax.
- **Specific categories:** The statute lists fifteen illustrative categories (not an exhaustive list) that help identify what constitutes gross income.
- **Exclusions:** Items specifically excluded elsewhere in the Internal Revenue Code (e.g., gifts, inheritances, certain scholarships, employer‑provided health insurance under § 105) are not part of gross income even though they might fall within the general phrasing.
- **Judicial interpretation:** Courts have upheld the expansive reading, treating the list as illustrative and emphasizing Congress’s intent to tax all accessions to wealth unless clearly exempt.
- **Practice tip:** When analyzing whether a receipt is taxable, first ask whether it falls within § 61’s broad definition; if yes, then look for a specific exclusion or exemption elsewhere in the Code.

## Key Cases & Authority

- **Commissioner v. Glenshaw Glass Co.**, 348 U.S. 426 (1955) – Defined “gross income” as accessions to wealth, clearly realized, and over which the taxpayer has complete dominion.  
- **Cesarini v. United States**, 296 F. Supp. 3 (N.D. Ohio 1968) – Found money found inside a purchased piano to be taxable income under § 61.  
- **James v. United States**, 366 U.S. 213 (1961) – Embezzled funds are taxable as gross income in the year received.  
- **U.S. v. Burke**, 504 U.S. 229 (1992) – Back pay for discrimination settlements is taxable as wages under § 61(a)(1).  
- **Old Colony Trust Co. v. Commissioner**, 279 U.S. 716 (1929) – Employer‑paid employee taxes constitute additional compensation to the employee.
