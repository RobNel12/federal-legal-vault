# I.R.C. § 165 – Losses

**Text:** (a) General Rule. – There shall be allowed as a deduction any loss sustained during the taxable year and not compensated for by insurance or otherwise.
(b) Amount of deduction. – For purposes of subsection (a), the basis for determining the amount of the loss for any loss described in paragraph (2) shall be the adjusted basis provided in section 1011 for determining the loss from the sale or other disposition of property.

**Subsections:**
- (c) Limitations on losses – includes wash sales (§ 1091), limitations on capital losses (§ 1211), and limitations on losses from passive activities (§ 469).
- (d) Capital losses – defined in § 1222.
- (e) Losses from wagering transactions – allowed only to the extent of winnings.
- (f) Abandonment losses – treated as a sale or exchange.

**Key Points:**
- Loss must be “sustained” – manifested in a closed and completed transaction, fixed by identifiable event, and actually suffered during the taxable year (*North American Oil Consolidated v. Burnet*, 286 U.S. 417 (1932)).
- Must be incurred in a trade or business, or in a transaction entered into for profit (though personal casualty losses are subject to special rules).
- Limitations: §§ 1211 (capital loss limitation), 469 (passive activity losses), 1091 (wash sale), 267 (related party losses).

**Landmark Cases:**
- *United States v. Kirby Lumber Co.*, 284 U.S. 1 (1931) – discharge of indebtedness income vs. loss.
- *Helvering v. Bruun*, 309 U.S. 461 (1940) – realization of gain when landlord receives improvement to property.
- *Cottage Savings Ass’n v. Commissioner*, 499 U.S. 554 (1991) – exchange of mortgages not a realization event; loss not recognized.
- *Commissioner v. Groetzinger*, 480 U.S. 23 (1987) – also relevant to loss deductions for gambling.
